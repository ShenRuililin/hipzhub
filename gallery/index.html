They Are Artists, Too

In Hong Kong, Filipino domestic workers are a ubiquitous presence, yet they often fade into the background of our daily lives. We encounter them in markets, on buses, and in the intimacy of our homes, where they care for our children and elderly with unwavering dedication. Despite their integral role in keeping the city's households running smoothly, we seldom pause to consider the depth of their experiences or the richness of their inner worlds.

The art created by these domestic workers serves as a powerful antidote to this invisibility, revealing a range of emotions and experiences that challenge our preconceptions. Their works, often created in precious moments between duties, showcase an remarkable range of qualities: resilience in the face of separation from loved ones, ingenuity in making art with limited resources, and a profound capacity for joy despite challenging circumstances. Through various mediums, they explore themes of identity, belonging, and the complexities of transnational families, offering nuanced perspectives on the migrant experience that go far beyond simple narratives of sacrifice or victimhood.

Their art forces us to pause. To really see the people we invite into our homes. To recognize their struggles, their dreams, their humanity. It's easy to take domestic workers for granted. Their art doesn't let us. It demands that we look closer, listen harder, and think deeper about the true cost of care.
